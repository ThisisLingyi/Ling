From two product lines to one customer domain.

Post-acquisition, Splunk’s direction was unification, but Data Management research was still split across Splunk Core and observability. I mapped the jobs users hire both lines to perform, showed through synthesis where those jobs are shared at the top and diverge below, and built gap analysis and satisfaction scoring so PMs and designers could plan a unified DM experience without a one-size-fits-all UX.

01 · Unification without alignment

Company direction vs. research reality

After AppDynamics moved into Splunk, unifying the data management experience became a company-level direction, not a nice-to-have. Customers were already treating ingestion, routing, governance, and observability data as one domain. Internally, it was still two product worlds with diverging research stories.

Guided data ingestion (internal program: GDI) had been a top pain point for more than five years. The gap wasn’t missing research, it was parallel JTBD work in isolation: stakeholders exploring their own job maps, personas, journeys, and service maps without a shared source of truth.

What “Data Management” means at Splunk

DM is the end-to-end experience of getting organizational data into, through, and governed within the platform so teams can search, monitor, and act on it:

DM areaWhat users are trying to do
Data ingestion & onboardingConnect sources, configure pipelines, get data to the right destinations.
Data processing & routingParse, transform, route, and enrich data through the platform.
Agent & connector managementDeploy, monitor, and maintain collection agents across hybrid and cloud.
Data governance & cost controlFilter noise, manage retention, control ingest volume, stay within license.
Cross-platform data experienceAlign data flows across Splunk Core and O11y (AppDynamics), especially post-integration.

The situation when I stepped in

  • Splunk Core and O11y research had diverged: same customers, different internal narratives.
  • PM, Design, Eng, Sales, Marketing, and Executives were planning and messaging from misaligned artifacts.
  • Teams knew what they were building but not which customer jobs were underserved across the full DM domain.
  • Post-acquisition unification pressure was rising, but there was no comparable job-map foundation across both lines to decide what to unify.

What was at stake: Duplicate interviews, conflicting prioritization, and a compromised “unified” UX that served neither forwarder-first nor OTel-first paths, while customers still experienced DM as one broken handoff chain.

03 · Foundational JTBD for two product lines

What I was trying to learn

Before anyone could unify DM in one place, both product lines needed the same question answered from the user’s side.

What jobs is Data Management hired to perform, and at what levels (ultimate → big → little)?

I ran a foundational JTBD program, not a single study:

  • Splunk Core guided-ingestion job map: platform onboarding path.
  • O11y guided-ingestion job map: observability onboarding path.
  • ~179 hours of JTBD research (interviews, synthesis, artifact build; aggregate fieldwork).
  • Consistent map structure so teams could compare apples-to-apples.
Methods: JTBD-informed interviews with admins, engineers, architects, and security stakeholders · multi-level job mapping · personas/mindsets · journey maps · monthly roadshow share-outs

Program design: six-month intensive roadmap

Within the longer post-acquisition timeline, I ran a six-month foundational roadmap in three phases.

Phase 01 · Foundation
Months 1–2

JTBD interviews; build both job maps.

Core + O11y maps · personas · initial journeys
Phase 02 · Alignment
Months 3–4

Cross-product synthesis; monthly share-outs.

Side-by-side comparison · Design/PM job alignment · exec readouts
Phase 03 · Action
Months 5–6

Gap analysis workshops; opportunity scoring.

Satisfaction scores · backlog-ready gaps · field-ready pain themes

One moment on the map

Ultimate job

Ensure the right organizational data is available in the platform for search, analytics, and operational use cases, reliably and cost-effectively.

Big job

Onboard and maintain data sources across a complex enterprise environment.

Little job (example)

Separate signal from noise before ingestion, so teams aren’t paying to index low-value telemetry.

Emotional job

Feel confident explaining ingestion choices to finance and security, not blindsided by cost or compliance surprises.

04 · Comparison showed shared jobs, and where to fork

The turning point

Once both maps existed, I ran cross-product synthesis, the core argument for why DM should live in one unified research and product strategy.

Similarities (why unification makes sense)

  • Same persona mindsets doing core functional jobs, with similar aspirations.
  • Same struggles: unclear ownership, manual processes, dependency on third-party tools and field help when gaps appear.
  • Same outcome expected: data reliably in the system, enabling day-to-day work.
  • Same emotional and social jobs: competence, credibility, not being blamed for outages.

Differences (why one-size UX fails)

DimensionSplunk CoreO11y
Data typesLogs, metrics (limited)Metrics, distributed traces
Connection methodsUniversal / heavy forwarderOpenTelemetry
Processing needsMore processing stepsFewer processing steps
Agent managementLimited native toolingRicher observability agent lifecycle
Implication shared with product and design

Customers experience one Data Management domain. The ultimate-job narrative should be unified; little jobs and journeys must fork where technical reality diverges.

That reframed post-acquisition direction in research terms: unification wasn’t “one wizard for everyone,” it was one job language, two execution paths where OTel-first and forwarder-first workflows differ.

05 · Gap analysis made maps actionable

The risk I named early

Job maps alone don’t create action. In program roadshows, the recurring risk was clear: teams would admire the map and still argue from gut feel, unclear how to move from job-map altitude to roadmap altitude.

Mitigation: feature gap analysis with user satisfaction scoring, piloted with designers and expanded to PMs.

The gap framework (per little job)

ColumnQuestionWho owns input
Current featuresWhat exists today that supports this job?Eng validates
Missing / in flightWhat’s being built vs. still absent?PM owns
User satisfactionHow satisfied are users with how well we support this job?Research + Design score
Design nextWhat should be designed or changed?Design translates
Field realityDoes this gap match what Sales/Marketing hear?GTM pressure-tests
Investment priorityWhere do execs invest given satisfaction + importance?Executives in tradeoff reviews

That template was used in workshops as a bridge from research library to roadmap discussions to go-to-market alignment, not a one-off exercise.

From satisfaction to opportunity scoring

Gap workshops fed a Job Opportunity Score, combining job importance, user difficulty, and satisfaction gaps to make portfolio tradeoffs visible in exec readouts. Planned enablement followed: job stories in PRDs, design pilots, and a JTBD resource hub so teams could self-serve the library.

Example (public-safe, illustrative): Pre-ingestion filtering and cost preview scored consistently low on satisfaction in gap workshops; participants described pre-processing in external tools because in-product controls arrived too late. This little job was discussed in prioritization conversations with PM and Design alongside eng feasibility, not presented here as a confirmed ship decision.

06 · What research informed (not product commitments)

Framed as research contribution and planning input. Product and roadmap decisions are owned by cross-functional partners.

Finding A · Unified narrative, forked execution

Tension  Pressure to ship one unified onboarding experience across Splunk Core and O11y post-integration.

Recommendation  Shared ultimate-job narrative plus product-specific little-job flows and journeys, avoiding a compromised one-size UX that served neither OTel-first nor forwarder-first paths.

Contribution  Cross-product comparison gave teams an evidence-backed unification model aligned with post-acquisition direction, without pretending technical paths were identical.

Finding B · Prioritization from gap scores, not feature requests alone

Tension  Connector expansion and UI polish competed in the same planning cycles.

Recommendation  Use gap analysis and satisfaction scores to surface consistently underserved little jobs (e.g., pre-ingestion filtering and cost preview) in portfolio discussions.

Contribution  Workshops moved conversations from “what did we hear last week?” to which little jobs score lowest on satisfaction, with PM, Design, and Eng validating feasibility in the same session.

Finding C · Research library as cross-org infrastructure

Tension  Parallel JTBD efforts, duplicated interviews, and conflicting narratives across product and GTM.

Recommendation  One domain library (job maps, journeys, personas, gap artifacts, monthly share-outs) as a shared starting point for PM, Design, Eng, Sales, Marketing, and Executives.

Contribution  Sole domain ownership plus roadshow cadence helped distribute the library beyond research; field and marketing partners revised artifacts as the domain evolved.

07 · What changed (impact)

Research outcomes and observed use in cross-functional planning, not verified product ship claims.

  • Built Splunk Core and O11y guided-ingestion job maps and synthesized a unified DM job narrative with forked little jobs, giving post-acquisition unification an evidence base from the user’s side.
  • Completed ~179 hours of foundational JTBD research and ran a six-month program (Foundation → Alignment → Action), socialized through monthly DM share-outs.
  • Introduced gap analysis and satisfaction scoring as a repeatable path from job maps to backlog and leadership tradeoff discussions.
  • Aligned Design and PM to jobs in workshops, replacing opinion-led debates with scored little jobs on a shared map.
  • Informed downstream strategic work, AI-Guided Onboarding: JTBD named the job before trust research scoped how AI could serve DM.
08 · Reflection

What I’d do again: map both product lines before arguing for unification. Comparison, not assertion, is what turned company direction into a product strategy teams could execute.

Role evolution I’d name earlier: supporting one product’s DM gave me credibility; owning both lines gave me mandate. Staff-level domain research often starts narrow and expands, tell that arc explicitly.

Gap analysis from day one: job maps earn admiration; feature audits and satisfaction scores earn backlog movement. I’d pilot gap workshops one phase earlier.

Why this layer matters for AI: you can’t judge whether AI solves a real problem until you’ve named the job, the little jobs, and where product already falls short. This foundation kept later AI work honest, not “splash AI on onboarding,” but “which handoff can AI actually shorten?”

Sole-researcher tradeoff: I could hold the full domain narrative, but monthly share-outs and revision cycles with PM, design, field, and exec partners distributed ownership so the library stayed current outside the research org.

What this case proves: I build research infrastructure at domain scale, foundational JTBD across two product lines, synthesis for unification, and an operating system (gap + satisfaction + scoring) that lets an entire org plan from the same customer jobs.
